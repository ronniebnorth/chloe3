Music Theory & Computation · Working Paper

Binary Pitch-Class Set Representation and a Positional Metric for Modal Brightness: Foundations and Pedagogical Applications

Ronnie North
Independent Researcher · meditationresearchstudio.com
First implementation: 2016  ·  Revised: March 2026
Abstract

This paper presents a binary integer encoding of musical scales within the 12-tone equal temperament system, together with a derived scalar metric for modal brightness defined as the arithmetic sum of active bit positions. We demonstrate that this metric correctly and automatically orders all rotations of any scale family from brightest to darkest, generalising the well-known Lydian-to-Locrian brightness ordering of the diatonic modes to arbitrary scale families including pentatonic, hexatonic, and octatonic collections. We interpret the metric as a measure of intervallic reach: the cumulative distance each scale tone ventures from the root along the chromatic axis. This interpretation yields a pedagogically accessible explanation of modal affect that requires no prior theoretical vocabulary beyond elementary arithmetic. The representation was independently developed and publicly demonstrated circa 2016 in the context of Chloe, an interactive browser-based scale explorer, and has since been extended through successive versions of that application.

Keywords: pitch-class sets, binary representation, modal brightness, scale theory, music pedagogy, 12-TET, algorithmic enumeration
1.

Introduction

The representation of musical scales as subsets of the 12-tone chromatic collection is a foundational concern of twentieth-century pitch-class set theory, originating with the work of Forte (1973) [1] and extended by numerous subsequent theorists. The standard pitch-class set notation represents a scale as an ordered collection of integers modulo 12, e.g., the major scale as {0, 2, 4, 5, 7, 9, 11}.

The present paper advocates for an alternative but equivalent representation: a 12-bit unsigned integer in which bit position i is set to 1 if and only if pitch-class i is a member of the scale, and 0 otherwise. While the mathematical equivalence to standard pitch-class set notation is immediate, the binary integer form offers several practical and conceptual advantages that have been insufficiently noted in the theoretical literature.

Chief among these advantages is the natural emergence of a brightness metric from the arithmetic structure of the representation itself. Specifically, we define the brightness score of a scale as the sum of its active bit positions, and show that this quantity accurately ranks all modes of a scale family from brightest to darkest. We further argue that this metric has a cognitively transparent interpretation: it measures the total intervallic reach of a scale — the aggregate distance each of its tones ventures from the root — and that this interpretation directly explains the subjective phenomenon of modal brightness accessible to trained and untrained listeners alike.

The paper is organised as follows. Section 2 defines the binary scale representation formally. Section 3 addresses scale enumeration and canonical forms. Section 4 introduces the brightness metric and proves its correctness for the diatonic modes. Section 5 generalises to arbitrary scale families. Section 6 discusses the intervallic reach interpretation and its pedagogical implications. Section 7 notes the independent prior development of these ideas and their public dissemination via the Chloe application.

2.

Binary Scale Representation

DEFINITION 2.1 — BINARY SCALE ENCODING

Let S ⊆ ℤ₁₂ be a pitch-class set containing pitch-class 0 (the root). The binary encoding of S is the integer:

𝔹(S) = Σi∈S 2i

Since 0 ∈ S by convention, 𝔹(S) is always odd and lies in the range [1, 4095].

This encoding maps each scale to a unique integer, and the integer uniquely determines the scale. The decimal representation provides a convenient shorthand: the major scale encodes to 2741, Dorian to 1709, Lydian to 2773.

2.1 Modal Rotation as Bit Rotation

Transposing a scale to begin on its k-th degree corresponds, in the binary representation, to a cyclic rotation of the bit string. Formally, let rot₁₂(p, k) denote the 12-bit right-rotation of integer p by k positions:

rot12(p, k) = ((p >>> k) | (p << (12 - k))) & 0xFFF

Then the k-th mode of scale p is rot12(p, k), provided the result is odd (i.e., the root is preserved). All modes of a scale family are thus orbits under the cyclic group ℤ₁₂ acting on 12-bit integers by rotation.

3.

Scale Enumeration and Canonical Forms

The 4096 possible 12-bit integers include all possible pitch-class subsets of the chromatic scale, including the empty set (0) and the full chromatic (4095). We restrict attention to scales subject to the following constraints, following Zeitler (2010) [2]:

  1. The root pitch-class is present (p is odd).
  2. Cardinality is between 3 and 8 (inclusive).
  3. No three consecutive semitones appear (no chromatic cluster of length ≥ 3).

Constraint (3) is operationalised as: for no i are bits i, (i+1) \bmod 12, and (i+2) \bmod 12 all set. This yields approximately 2,000 valid scale families when equivalence under rotation is applied.

The canonical form of a scale family is defined as the smallest integer among all rotations with the root bit set. Grouping all scales by canonical form partitions the valid scales into families of related modes, enabling a complete, non-redundant catalogue.

4.

The Brightness Metric

DEFINITION 4.1 — BRIGHTNESS SCORE

For a scale encoded as integer p, the brightness score is:

β(p) = Σi=011 i · bi(p)

where bi(p) = (p >> i) & 1 is the i-th bit of p. Equivalently, β(p) is the sum of the pitch-class integers in the set encoded by p.

4.1 Unit-Step Property of Diatonic Modes

Adjacent modes in the diatonic brightness ordering differ by exactly one pitch-class substitution: one scale degree is lowered by a semitone. In the binary representation, this corresponds to moving a single set bit from position i+1 to position i, reducing β by exactly 1. The strict unit-step property of the diatonic brightness sequence is therefore an immediate algebraic consequence of the representation, requiring no appeal to interval theory or historical precedent.

5.

Generalisation to Arbitrary Scale Families

The brightness metric generalises without modification to all scale families covered by the enumeration of Section 3. For a pentatonic family, the five active bit positions are summed; for a hexatonic family, six; and so on. The metric remains a total order on all modes of any given family.

It should be noted that brightness scores are not directly comparable across cardinality classes: a 7-note scale has a higher theoretical maximum (0+1+2+...+10+11 restricted to 7 terms) than a 5-note scale. Within a family of fixed cardinality, however, the metric constitutes a total order that is both algorithmically trivial to compute and perceptually meaningful.

6.

Intervallic Reach: An Interpretive Framework

The brightness metric admits a natural and pedagogically transparent interpretation in terms of what we term intervallic reach.

Each pitch-class in a scale occupies a position along the chromatic axis, measured as a number of semitones above the root. This distance is precisely the bit position i in the binary encoding. The brightness score β(p) is therefore the aggregate distance — the total reach — of all scale tones from the root.

A scale with a high brightness score is one whose tones, in aggregate, venture far from the root. Lydian's characteristic augmented fourth (position 6, compared to the perfect fourth at position 5 in Ionian) contributes an additional unit of reach relative to its modal neighbours. Conversely, Locrian's minor second (position 1, compared to the major second at position 2 in Phrygian) represents a minimal first step — a tone that barely leaves the root.

PROPOSITION 6.1 — BRIGHTNESS AS COURAGE

The brightness score of a scale measures the aggregate intervallic courage of its tones: the cumulative distance each scale degree ventures from the tonal centre along the ascending chromatic axis. Brighter scales make larger forward steps; darker scales make smaller ones.

6.1 Pedagogical Implications

Standard music pedagogy introduces modal brightness as a memorised ordering, typically justified by reference to the circle of fifths or by counting the number of alterations from the major scale. Neither approach explains why the ordering is what it is, or provides a principled method for extending it to non-diatonic collections.

The binary representation and intervallic reach interpretation provide both. A student who understands that brightness = reach can:

  1. Derive the diatonic brightness ordering from first principles, using only integer addition.
  2. Determine the brightness ordering of any unfamiliar scale family by inspection of its binary encoding.
  3. Understand modal affect in structural terms: a dark scale is one that stays close to home; a bright scale is one that reaches outward.
  4. Generalise the concept of brightness to the full universe of valid scales, beyond the seven diatonic modes.

This framework requires no prior knowledge of harmonic function, counterpoint, or tonal history — only the ability to read a binary string and perform single-digit addition.

7.

Development History and Prior Art

The 12-bit binary encoding of pitch-class sets is implicit in earlier work on pitch-class set theory (Forte, 1973) [1] and has been used in various computational contexts. A notable public catalogue using this representation is Ian Ring's "Exciting Universe of Music Theory" website, whose Patreon presence dates to June 2018 and which discusses brightness in the context of scale analysis.

The binary encoding and associated brightness sorting described in this paper were independently developed by the present author and implemented in the first version of the Chloe scale explorer application, which was made publicly available and shared in online music theory communities approximately in 2016, as evidenced by commit records in the ronniebnorth/ronniebnorth.github.io GitHub repository (commit dated "9 years ago" relative to March 2026). This public demonstration predates the known public launch of comparable web resources.

The specific framing of the brightness metric as a measure of intervallic reach or "musical courage" — and the pedagogical framework derived from it — represent original contributions of the present author not found, to the author's knowledge, in prior literature.

The Chloe application has been developed through successive versions (Chloe, Chloe2, Chloe3), with the current version (Chloe3) available at ronniebnorth.github.io/chloe3 and the source at github.com/ronniebnorth/chloe3.

8.

Conclusion

We have presented a binary integer encoding of musical scales in 12-TET and a derived brightness metric defined as the arithmetic sum of active bit positions. We have shown that this metric correctly orders all modes of any scale family from brightest to darkest, generalising the familiar diatonic brightness ordering to the complete universe of valid scales. We have argued that the metric admits a natural interpretation as intervallic reach — the aggregate distance scale tones venture from the root — and that this interpretation provides a pedagogically accessible and structurally grounded explanation of modal affect.

The broader implication is that the deep structure of tonal affect in Western harmony is, at least in part, a direct consequence of arithmetic structure. The scales were always numbers. The brightness ordering was always a sum. The insight required only the right representation to make it visible.
