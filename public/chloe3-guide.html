Chapter 1
What is Chloe?

A musical scale is a set of notes chosen from the 12 semitones of the chromatic scale. Western music mostly uses two of them — the major scale and the minor scale — but there are thousands of others, each with its own mood, colour, and cultural history.

Chloe generates every mathematically valid scale from 3 to 8 notes and lets you hear them all. It filters out scales with three consecutive semitones (which tend to sound like chromatic noise rather than a scale), leaving a catalogue of musically interesting structures, including many you'll recognise and many you won't have encountered before.

The core idea: Drone a root note and step through a scale. Listen to what makes Lydian sound dreamy, Phrygian sound tense, a Japanese pentatonic sound sparse, or an octatonic diminished scale sound like a film score. Chloe makes that exploration immediate and tactile.

All sound is synthesised live in your browser using the Web Audio API — no samples, no downloads, no plugins. Open the page and it just works.

1.1
The scale catalogue

Scales are organised into families. A family is a group of scales that share the same interval structure — they're rotations of each other, the way C major and D Dorian are both rotations of the same seven-note pattern. Each rotation is called a mode.

Within a family, modes are sorted from brightest to darkest. Brightness is calculated by summing the semitone positions of the active notes — more sharps means brighter. This naturally produces the familiar Lydian → Ionian → Mixolydian → Dorian → Aeolian → Phrygian → Locrian ordering for the seven standard modes.

Families are named by note count and a number: hep-6 means the 6th heptatonic (7-note) family, which is the standard diatonic family containing all the church modes.

Chapter 2
Binary Notation

Every scale in Chloe is represented as a 12-bit binary string — one bit for each semitone of the chromatic scale, starting from the root note. A 1 means that semitone is active in the scale; a 0 means it's skipped.

Reading from left to right: bit 0 is the root (always 1), bit 1 is one semitone up, bit 2 is a whole tone, and so on up to bit 11 (a major seventh).

The binary string is 101011010101, which equals 2741 in decimal. This number is a compact, unique fingerprint for each scale — independent of root note or key, since it describes the interval pattern, not the specific pitches.

2.1
The scale wheel

Chloe displays each selected scale as a polygon on a chromatic circle — twelve points arranged clockwise, one per semitone. Active scale tones are highlighted in amber and connected by lines.

The shape of the polygon gives you an immediate visual feel for the scale's character:

Sparse scales
Wide polygons
Pentatonics produce a loose, open shape — five widely spaced points. Easy to navigate, forgiving, a lot of air between notes.
Dense scales
Compact polygons
Octatonic and chromatic-heavy scales produce tight, almost circular shapes. More tension, more colour, less obvious resolution.

The root note is always at the top. Rotating the root (using the key selector) rotates the whole polygon — the shape stays the same because the interval structure doesn't change.

Chapter 3
Playback

Chloe's playback is designed to let you hear a scale in context rather than just reading its notes. There are several layers you can combine freely.

3.1
Drone

The drone sustains the root note continuously while you explore the scale above it. This is the most direct way to hear the modal character of a scale — each note in the arpeggio or melody is heard against a fixed tonal centre, making the tensions and resolutions audible rather than theoretical.

The DRONE OCT control drops the drone 1, 2, or 3 octaves below the playing register, giving it weight as a bass foundation. The DRONE VOL slider sets its level independently of the notes.

3.2
Arpeggio and Melody

The Play button starts stepping through the scale. It has two modes:

Mode
Arpeggio
Steps through scale notes in order — ascending, descending, or random. Predictable and pattern-focused. Direction is set by the arrow controls.
Mode
Melody
A weighted random walk — favours stepwise motion but occasionally leaps an octave or reverses direction. More organic and phrase-like.
3.3
Rhythm

The RHYTHM control sets the note-spacing pattern for arpeggio mode. All rhythms are BPM-synced at eighth-note resolution.

3.5
Beat

The Beat button adds a percussion track — kick, snare, and hi-hat — synthesised live in the browser (no samples). It runs independently of the arpeggio so you can combine them freely.

The beat automatically follows the RHYTHM setting: Even gives a straight rock beat, Swing adds triplet groove, Gallop drives a double-kick pattern, Waltz plays in 3/4, and Clave runs the son clave rhythm at 16th-note resolution. BPM changes take effect on the next step.

Chapter 4
Sound

All audio is synthesised in real time using the Web Audio API. There are no sample files — every sound is built from oscillators, noise, filters, and envelopes in JavaScript.

Instrument
Piano
Three detuned triangle oscillators with a second and third harmonic overlay. Low-pass filtered with a sharp exponential decay. Sounds clean and bell-like.
Instrument
Guitar
Karplus-Strong synthesis — a noise burst fed into a delay loop tuned to the target frequency. Produces a plucked string character.
Instrument
Xylo
Multiple sine partials tuned to marimba-like ratios (1×, 4×, 10×…) with staggered decays. Bright, percussive, idiomatic for scale runs.
Instrument
Space
Two detuned sine oscillators with LFO vibrato. Slow attack, long release. The drone in space mode is particularly effective for meditative listening.

The beat track runs dry by design — it bypasses reverb and delay so the kick and snare stay punchy and present while the melodic layer can be as wet and spacious as you like.
4.2
Timbre

For the piano, guitar, and xylo instruments, the TIMBRE control shifts the oscillator waveform between sine, triangle, sawtooth, and square — from warm and smooth to bright and buzzy. Less relevant for guitar (which uses noise synthesis) and disabled for the space instrument, which has its own fixed character.

Chapter 5
Auto & Demo Modes
5.1
Auto mode

The Auto button makes Chloe explore autonomously — cycling through named scales every 12–16 seconds, randomising root note, rhythm, arpeggio direction, chord voicing, and BPM each time. It covers the full catalogue of 45 named scales, always choosing something different from the current scale.

This is useful as a background listening mode, or as a way to quickly survey scales you haven't spent time with. The current scale and settings appear in the status banner.

5.2
Demo mode — Claude AI

The Claude button enables AI-driven exploration. This uses the Claude Haiku model via the Anthropic API to make musical choices — not just randomising, but actively thinking about contrast, variety, and musical interest as it moves between scales.

Each turn, Claude receives the current state (scale, root, rhythm, BPM), the last three scales it played, and the full catalogue of named scales with their interval patterns. The history lets it contrast musically — varying brightness, note density, and feel rather than wandering randomly. It returns a JSON choice including a short piece of commentary on the scale's character, which appears in the banner.

★ DEMO Lydian Dominant — a Mixolydian with a raised fourth, sitting
between brightness and blues tension. Overtone series in miniature.

Claude can also leave feature requests — if it notices something missing or imagines a capability the app doesn't have, it can write a brief note in amber alongside its commentary. These requests accumulate in the demo log so you can review what it's been asking for over a session.

API key required. Click ★ Claude and enter an Anthropic API key (available at console.anthropic.com under API Keys — this requires separate billing from Claude.ai subscriptions). Your key is stored in your browser's local storage only and is never sent anywhere except directly to the Anthropic API.
5.3
The demo log

Both Auto and Demo modes maintain a log of recent scale choices, accessible via the ▾ log button in the banner. Each entry shows the scale name, root note, BPM, rhythm, and commentary. Clicking any entry replays that exact combination.

Chapter 6
Sharing & URL State

Every setting in Chloe is encoded into the URL. The Share button (top right) builds a URL encoding the current scale, root note, instrument, BPM, tuning, rhythm, chord voicing, arpeggio direction, reverb, delay, and sidebar width — then copies it to your clipboard.

Paste or bookmark the URL to restore the exact session later, or send it to someone else so they hear exactly what you were hearing. The URL is human-readable: each parameter has a short key, and the values are compact enough that the full URL stays shareable.

https://ronniebnorth.github.io/chloe3/?
sel=hep-6.3&r=7&i=piano&b=112&rh=swing&cv=triad&rv=0.30
